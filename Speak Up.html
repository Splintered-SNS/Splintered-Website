Speak Up

A major contributor to the rise of mental illnesses is the lack of discussion about them. In the past, it was always suggested that one should keep their feelings to themselves to avoid ridicule. Now the same seems like a compulsion.

Mental health is not taken seriously in India. There is a lot of stigma surrounding the concept. Mental illnesses are not treated the way physical illnesses are. Being healthy is only thought of as physical health, whereas in reality, health is when both your physical body and mental state are in harmony. Unfortunately, people are still casually termed “paagal” (mad) when associated with depression, even by educated people. Bollywood recently came out with a film titled, “Mental Hai Kya”, which was changed to “Judgemental Hai Kya” after a big commotion.

This highlights the mentality of our society. This is why India is shortlisted amongst the countries with the maximum number of depressed people. Many people have mental health issues due to their work life. Illnesses like depression are not taken seriously. Individuals with depression are considered either needy or ungrateful. Perhaps they simply require someone to talk to. It is necessary to be receptive to someone who has mustered the courage to open up.

This is why many people aren't comfortable speaking to their parents or friends about their struggles. We created this website to act as a platform for you to voice your struggle. On this page, you can share your stories. Even if we can’t help you in person, we are ready to provide moral support. Remember, the more you internalize your issues, the more harmful they become for you.

A Story

Here’s a story of our friend who was recently diagnosed with OCD (Obsessive-compulsive disorder)

Sanjana was diagnosed with OCD. She had many obsessions, including ones related to contamination (fear of contracting HIV), aggressions (intrusive image of hitting someone) and symmetry (exactness in the level of water).

“People don't know what it's like; they often loosely term the illness thinking it is no big deal. Whereas, in reality they don't know what it's like. It’s so hard to go out and touch anything; from groceries to using the metro, it's all like a quest. On top of that I have people making fun of how obsessive I get with something or the kind of anxiety I face because of my illness. I wish someone would just try to understand what I am going through.”
